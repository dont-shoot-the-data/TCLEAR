Every major institution examining fatal violence against transgender Americans has converged on a single narrative: these killings represent a hate-driven “epidemic” fueled by societal transphobia, systemic racism, and legislative hostility. The Human Rights Campaign, whose annual victim tracking lists serve as the foundational data source for virtually all institutional statements on this subject, explicitly attributed the violence to “white supremacy” through its president, Alphonso David, in both a public statement and written congressional testimony in 2020. U.S. Senator Cory Booker blamed “a white supremacist system.” The American Medical Association declared a “public health crisis.” A former President named a transgender murder suspect alongside George Floyd as a victim of police violence. 465 celebrities signed an open letter blaming “a hostile climate of anti-LGBTQ rhetoric.” Despite publishing the names and stories of every victim for over a decade, HRC never once reported the demographic identity of the people actually committing these homicides. This study is the first to independently verify every case on HRC's annual tracking lists from 2015 to 2024 against primary sources (court records, local news coverage, law enforcement statements, and autopsy reports) and produce a case-level dataset with suspect demographics, independent bias motive coding, circumstance categorization, and case outcomes across a full decade (N = 304 victims; N = 186 identified suspects).

The findings contradict the institutional consensus at virtually every level. Black suspects account for 122 of 188 identified perpetrators (64.9%), nearly five times their 13.6% share of the U.S. population. White suspects account for 34 of 188 (18.1%), less than one-third their 59.3% population share. This pattern held in every single year: in no year from 2015 to 2024 did white suspects exceed half the number of Black suspects. In 2020, the year when many major institutions blamed white supremacy and systemic racism, zero White suspects were identified. Approximately 80% of solved cases are intraracial, mirroring national FBI homicide data exactly. Only 11 of 302 coded cases (3.6%) resulted in confirmed hate crime determinations. The #1 identified motive is intimate partner and domestic violence (26 of 121 known-motive cases), not bias. The #2 motive is sexual encounters and sex work (20 cases). The #3 motive is disputes and arguments (18 cases). Solve rates average 62%, at or above national benchmarks. Six police-involved killings occurred in ten years; every one was ruled justified. The overall transgender homicide rate is approximately one-third the general population rate.

These patterns are statistically indistinguishable from national homicide data. The “epidemic of anti-transgender hate violence” framing is not supported by the evidence. What the data reveals instead is interpersonal violence, primarily intimate partner violence, sex work encounters, disputes, and robberies, occurring within the same communities, following the same intraracial patterns that characterize American homicide broadly. No prior study has examined this data at the case level. The institutional consensus was built without it.

Since 2013, the Human Rights Campaign Foundation has published annual reports documenting fatal violence against transgender and gender-non-conforming people in the United States. These reports, titled “An Epidemic of Violence,” have become the authoritative source for virtually every institutional statement on the subject, from presidential proclamations to congressional resolutions to AMA policy declarations. The reports compile victim lists, memorialize the dead, and frame the violence as a product of societal transphobia, legislative hostility, and systemic racism.

What HRC's reports do not do, and what no prior study has done, is independently examine the data at the case level. HRC does not systematically report suspect demographics. It does not analyze motive evidence against a structured coding framework. It does not compare case outcomes to national benchmarks. It does not calculate solve rates or measure whether law enforcement is actually deprioritizing these cases. And it does not test whether the observed patterns differ from general homicide dynamics in any statistically meaningful way.

The result is that the most consequential institutional consensus on any social issue in recent American life, one that has shaped federal policy, medical association resolutions, congressional legislation, celebrity advocacy, and academic theory, was built on an evidence base that no one independently verified. This study fills that gap.

Using HRC's victim lists as the source framework but rejecting HRC's interpretive framing, this study independently verified each of the 304 cases documented between 2015 and 2024. For each case, primary sources (local news coverage, court records, police statements, and where available, autopsy reports) were used to extract suspect demographics, code bias motivation against a four-tier anchored framework, categorize circumstances, and track case outcomes. The result is the first case-level dataset of fatal anti-transgender violence in the United States that includes suspect race, motive coding, circumstance categorization, and longitudinal case tracking across a full decade.

The findings are reported below. They contradict the institutional consensus at every level tested.

Black White Hispanic Other

In Midtown Sacramento, on a building at 2013 Neighbors Alley between 19th and 21st Streets, there is a large public mural. On the right side, a portrait of a young Black woman above the words: “Protect Our Trans Daughters.” Below the portrait, the mural identifies its subject: Chyna Gibson, a Black transgender performer from Sacramento who was shot ten times and killed in a New Orleans parking lot on February 25, 2017, during Mardi Gras. The mural was created by artists Jessica Sabogal and Shanna Strauss to, in its own words, “document the epidemic of anti-transgender violence across the U.S.”

On the left side of the same mural, extending across the adjacent wall, is a second panel. It reads: “White Silence = Violence.” Below that: “Say Her Name.” And below that: “Am I Next?”

Here is what the data shows about Chyna Gibson’s case: The New Orleans Police Department identified two Black men as persons of interest in her killing. NOPD explicitly stated there was “nothing demonstrating that this is a hate crime.” The case remains unsolved. No white person has ever been identified as a suspect or person of interest in connection with her death.

And here is what the data shows about Chyna Gibson’s demographic category: In this dataset, 122 of 188 identified suspects in transgender homicides are Black (64.9%), nearly five times the Black share of the U.S. population. White suspects account for 34 of 188 (18.1%), less than one-third their population share. In 2020 alone, zero White suspects were identified. The #1 motive is intimate partner violence. The #2 motive is sex work encounters. Only 11 of 302 cases (3.6%) resulted in confirmed hate crime determinations. The violence is overwhelmingly intraracial: Black suspects killing Black transgender victims, in patterns statistically indistinguishable from American homicide broadly.

The mural on Neighbors Alley is not an outlier. It is the institutional consensus rendered in paint. A Black transgender woman is killed by what all available evidence suggests were Black perpetrators, in a case with zero evidence of anti-transgender bias, and the community response, endorsed and funded by advocacy organizations and municipal arts programs, is a public installation blaming white silence. The mural does not name a suspect. It does not mention intimate partner violence, or sex work, or the intraracial dynamics that define the overwhelming majority of these cases. It does what every institution documented in this study has done: it skips the data entirely and proceeds directly to the narrative.

Consider the reverse. If the data showed that white men were killing Black transgender women at five times their population share, and a mural appeared in a Sacramento alley reading “Black Silence = Violence,” the building would not survive the week. The phrase would be catalogued as a hate crime. The artists would be investigated. The city would issue a public apology. Every institution quoted in this study would mobilize against it. But when the actual perpetrators are disproportionately Black and the mural blames white people, no one objects. Not because no one knows. Because knowing would require doing something about it, and doing something about it would require saying something that no one in American public life is permitted to say.

That is the core finding of this study, and it extends well beyond statistics. The institutional consensus documented in the Literature Review was not built on ignorance. HRC had the victim data. Law enforcement had the suspect data. Court records were public. News articles named the suspects and published their photographs. The information was always available. What was missing was not data. What was missing was a single person in a position of institutional authority willing to say what the data showed. And the reason no one said it is because the incentive structure of American public life makes saying it professionally fatal. A researcher who publishes these findings risks being labeled racist, transphobic, or both. A journalist who reports them risks losing their career. A politician who acknowledges them risks losing their seat. An advocacy organization that confronts them risks losing its funding. The result is a system in which every institutional actor knows, or could know, that the narrative is wrong, but no one will say so because the personal cost of telling the truth exceeds the institutional cost of letting people die.

This is not an abstraction. These are human lives. Every year that the institutional consensus directs resources, funding, and public attention toward “white supremacy” and “legislative hostility” as the drivers of this violence is a year that resources are not directed toward intimate partner violence intervention, emergency housing for transgender people fleeing abusive relationships, and community-based safety programs in the neighborhoods where these killings actually occur. No problem in the history of the world has ever been solved by misdiagnosing it. You do not find a treasure with the wrong map. You do not cure a disease by treating the wrong organ. And you do not prevent the murders of Black transgender women by blaming white silence when the exposed data shows that Black men account for 122 of 188 identified suspects.

The people and institutions who most loudly proclaim their commitment to protecting transgender lives are the same people and institutions who have, for over a decade, refused to look at the data that would tell them how. They have read names on debate stages. They have signed open letters. They have declared epidemics and emergencies and crises. They have built murals. And in all that time, not one of them has said the single sentence that the data makes inescapable: the primary pattern in this dataset is Black men killing Black transgender women in the context of intimate relationships and sexual encounters, in patterns that are statistically indistinguishable from American homicide broadly. They have not said it because saying it would help no one’s political career, fund no one’s organization, advance no one’s tenure case, and win no one an award for moral courage. The ideology was always more important than the lives. The mural on Neighbors Alley is the proof.

The institutional consensus it critiques also treats these deaths as abstractions, deploying them as evidence for political arguments about white supremacy, legislative hostility, and systemic transphobia. In both cases, the human being disappears. This section exists to undo that. Malik Jackson was 21 years old. He lived in Tallahassee, Florida with his mother, Jennifer Jackson. He worked at Five Guys. His boss told his family that Malik would arrive before the restaurant opened, sitting in the parking lot waiting for someone to come unlock the door. He loved to fish. He loved to dance. His aunt, Abigail Jackson, described him simply: “Malik was a good kid. Malik didn’t get in any trouble. Malik smiled a lot. He really didn’t talk.”

Tony McDade was a 38-year-old Black transgender man. He had been released from a ten-year federal prison sentence for armed robbery in January 2020, four months before the events described here. In early 2020, McDade entered into a relationship with Jennifer Jackson, Malik’s mother and McDade’s neighbor. According to the Jackson family and police records, in the days before May 27, McDade pistol-whipped Jennifer in her own home, returned the following day and became verbally abusive, and sent threatening text messages including “I’ll kill for you and you.” On May 26, a confrontation escalated when Malik told McDade to leave and stop disrespecting his mother. A fight broke out. Jennifer called 911 but later told dispatchers police were not needed. Malik’s aunt Abigail called 911 separately, telling them McDade was “mentally ill, very disturbed, and had a knife and a gun.” Police came. Nothing happened.

On the morning of May 27, 2020, McDade posted a Facebook Live video. In it, he threatened to kill five men, including those involved in the previous day’s fight. “Y’all ain’t gone look the same when them bullets touch your dome,” he said. “Warning comes before destruction. And I’m telling you five motherf— that you’re going to die.”

Shortly after, McDade walked to Jennifer Jackson’s home. Malik was sitting in his car in the driveway, as he routinely did before heading to work. McDade approached the vehicle and stabbed him in the neck. Malik Jackson died at the hospital. He was alone when he died, because COVID-19 restrictions prevented his mother and aunt from entering the building, even as they begged to see him.

McDade fled on foot. Less than an hour later, Tallahassee police officers located McDade near the Leon Arms apartment complex. According to the Tallahassee Police Department, officers’ testimony, and body camera footage from other responding officers, McDade pulled out a handgun, took a shooting stance from approximately 10 to 15 yards away, and pointed the weapon at a responding officer. The officer fired seven shots. Three struck McDade, including one to the abdomen that was fatal. A gun and a bloody knife were recovered at the scene. On September 3, 2020, a Leon County grand jury found the use of force justified and issued a “No True Bill.”

That is what happened on May 27, 2020. Now here is what happened one week later.

Obama Foundation Town Hall, June 3, 2020 — “Reimagining Policing in the Wake of Continued Police Violence”

On June 3, 2020, former President Barack Obama hosted an online town hall through the Obama Foundation’s My Brother’s Keeper Alliance. The event was titled “Reimagining Policing in the Wake of Continued Police Violence.” In his opening remarks, Obama named the families of African Americans who had recently died at the hands of police. He named George Floyd. He named Breonna Taylor. He named Ahmaud Arbery. And he named Tony McDade (verified by The Advocate).

“Most of all, the pain that’s been experienced by the families of George and Breonna, Ahmaud, Tony and Sean, and too many others to mention,” Obama said. “To those families who’ve been directly affected by tragedy, please know that Michelle and I grieve with you and hold you in our prayers. We’re committed to the fight of creating a more just nation in memory of your sons and daughters.”

Tony McDade, who had that morning been released from a decade in federal prison. Who had pistol-whipped his girlfriend. Who had posted a public death threat on Facebook Live. Who had walked up to a 21-year-old sitting in his car and stabbed him to death. Who had then pulled a gun on police. Tony McDade was named by the 44th President of the United States, before an audience of millions, as a victim of police violence whose memory demanded the reimagining of American policing.

No one at the Obama Foundation checked the facts of the case before the former president said Tony McDade’s name. No one noted that McDade was a murder suspect who had pointed a firearm at a police officer. No one mentioned Malik Jackson at all. His name was not read. His family was not acknowledged. His mother, who was not allowed to hold her dying son’s hand because of a pandemic, watched a former president memorialize the man who killed him.

The Jackson family’s attorneys responded publicly: “We believe that if President Obama knew exactly what happened on May 26th and May 27th, he would not have made those statements of Tony McDade. And we believe an apology should be stated to the family of Malik Jackson.”

No apology was ever issued.

When a local activist was asked whether McDade’s actions, including the murder of Malik Jackson, should factor into the public response, she answered: “It doesn’t matter what he did.”

Malik Jackson’s aunt, Abigail Jackson, offered a different view: “Malik Jackson’s life mattered too.”

In this dataset, Tony McDade is coded as: Black transgender man, age 38, Tallahassee FL, police-involved shooting, use of force ruled justified, no evidence of anti-transgender bias. He is one of six police-involved cases in the ten-year dataset. Every one was ruled justified. He is also, through Barack Obama, one of the 20 institutional voices catalogued in the Literature Review of this study, because the former president used his death to advance the narrative that American policing is systemically violent toward transgender people of color.

Malik Jackson is not in this dataset at all. He was not transgender. He was a 21-year-old Black man who went to work every day and loved to fish and was murdered by someone the police had been called about the night before. His name was never read on a debate stage. No celebrity signed a letter in his memory. No president said his name. No mural was painted. The institutional consensus that this study documents had no use for Malik Jackson, because his death did not serve the narrative. He was the wrong kind of victim, killed by the wrong kind of person, in the wrong kind of story. So they said the name of the person who killed him instead, and they called it justice.

Prior Academic Research

Research on fatal violence against transgender people in the United States has been limited by data availability. Key prior contributions include:

  • Stotzer (2009) conducted one of the earliest systematic reviews, documenting violence against transgender individuals. Her key finding was that individuals known to the victims — not strangers — were physically and sexually assaulting transgender people at high rates, a result consistent with this study’s finding that intimate partner violence is the #1 identified circumstance. Stotzer also noted that existing data relied on advocacy reports and media coverage rather than criminal justice records, and that methodological limitations made it “impossible to determine causes or determinants of violence” — a gap this study directly addresses.
  • Dinno (2017) published one of the few peer-reviewed epidemiological analyses, estimating transgender homicide rates using population denominators and concluding that transgender people — particularly Black transgender women — face elevated homicide risk.
  • HRC Annual Reports (2013–present) provide the most widely cited annual tracking of fatal anti-transgender violence. These reports compile victim lists and generally frame the deaths as part of an “epidemic” of hate violence. However, HRC does not systematically analyze suspect demographics, motive evidence, or case outcomes — the reports primarily memorialize victims and advocate for policy responses. This study’s findings challenge the “epidemic” framing at virtually every level of analysis.
  • Gruberg et al. (2021, Center for American Progress) analyzed the intersection of economic marginalization and violence against transgender people, arguing that structural factors including poverty, housing instability, and survival sex work increase vulnerability to violence. This study did not measure economic conditions and therefore cannot confirm or refute these structural claims; however, the suspect demographic and circumstance data suggest that the proximate drivers of this violence are interpersonal — intimate partner conflict, sexual encounters, and disputes — rather than the structural or political forces emphasized in institutional framing.
  • FBI Hate Crime Statistics began including gender identity as a bias category in 2013. Federal data consistently shows a small number of gender-identity-motivated hate crimes reported annually, far fewer than advocacy estimates.

Gap This Study Fills

No prior study has independently verified each individual case on HRC’s victim lists against primary sources (court records, local news, law enforcement statements) to produce a case-level dataset with suspect demographics, verified bias motive coding, circumstance categorization, and case outcomes across a full decade. Existing literature has generally relied on one of two approaches: (1) advocacy reports that assume anti-transgender bias as the default motive, or (2) aggregate federal data (FBI UCR/NIBRS) that severely undercounts hate crimes due to voluntary reporting.

This study occupies a distinct middle ground: it uses HRC’s victim lists as the most comprehensive available source framework, but rejects HRC’s interpretive framing in favor of independent case-level analysis. The result is a dataset that can inform both advocacy and academic discussion with greater empirical precision than either approach alone.

The Institutional Consensus

For over a decade, the institutional narrative around fatal violence against transgender people has attributed this violence to white supremacy, societal transphobia, and legislative hostility. This framing has not been confined to any single sector. It has been advanced simultaneously by presidents and presidential candidates, by the U.S. Congress, by the American Medical Association, by the nation’s largest LGBTQ advocacy organizations, by Hollywood actors, producers, and showrunners, by peer-reviewed academic scholarship, and by mass-signed celebrity open letters. It constitutes what may be the most complete institutional consensus on any social issue in recent American life — and this dataset shows it is empirically wrong. The following is a representative but far from exhaustive catalogue:

  • HRC President Alphonso David (June 2020), in a statement explicitly connecting anti-transgender violence to white supremacy: “We must all demand change. That means changing the systems and structures. That means examining the ways that we, each of us — knowingly or unintentionally — support these systems of white supremacy through our actions, inactions, complicity, and indifference.” (HRC, “Black Lives Matter”)
  • HRC 2021 State Equality Index, framing transgender violence within a white supremacy framework: “The past year has underscored how white supremacy has a toxic grip on our democracy — a reality that too many of us have lived with for far too long.” (HRC 2021 State Equality Index)
  • Tori Cooper, HRC Transgender Justice Initiative Director (November 2023): “Almost two-thirds of the victims reported on were Black trans women, a tragedy that reflects an appalling trend of violence fueled by racism, toxic masculinity, trans misogynoir and transphobia.” (HRC 2023 Report)
  • President Barack Obama (June 3, 2020), at an Obama Foundation town hall titled “Reimagining Policing in the Wake of Continued Police Violence,” named Tony McDade alongside George Floyd, Breonna Taylor, and Ahmaud Arbery as victims of a system that “devalues” Black lives. Independent Verification: For each case, the researcher located primary source documentation (news reports, police press releases, court dockets, autopsy information where available) to verify victim identity, manner of death, suspect identity, case status, and documented circumstances. HRC’s own characterizations, bias assessments, and narrative framing were not used as data inputs.

Coding Framework:

Suspect Race/Ethnicity: Coded from mugshots, court records, news photography, and law enforcement physical descriptions. Categories: Black, White, Hispanic, Asian/Other, Unknown. Two suspects in 2015 were coded under two racial categories (Hispanic and White) because their ethnicity could not be reduced to a single group; they are counted once in each applicable category, yielding 188 racial classifications across 186 unique suspects.

Anti-Trans Bias Motive: Four-tier coding: Confirmed (hate crime charges filed or explicit law enforcement statement), Suspected (evidence of animus without formal charges), No Evidence (documented alternative motive), Unknown (insufficient information). HRC bias assessments were not used.

Case Status: Coded as Solved (arrest, conviction, or guilty plea), Unsolved (no suspect identified), or Pending (charges filed, awaiting trial). Solve rates are calculated as (cases with at least one identified suspect) divided by (total victims in that year), including police-involved cases in the denominator. This produces slightly more conservative solve rate figures than excluding police cases from the denominator.

Circumstances: Independently categorized from primary source documentation: intimate partner/domestic/family violence, sex work encounter, dispute/argument, robbery/theft, discovery/panic, drug-related, serial killing, police-involved, concealment of relationship, random/stranger, in custody, incidental, or unknown/unsolved.

Inter-Rater Reliability and Dual-Coding Disclosure

Dual-Coding Transparency: This study was conducted by a single researcher using AI-assisted analysis (Claude, Anthropic). All suspect race/ethnicity designations, bias motive codings, and case status determinations were produced through iterative human-AI review of primary sources. This methodology differs from traditional inter-rater reliability frameworks, which use two or more independent human coders.

Mitigation: To address this limitation, all suspect racial designations were independently audited against primary sources (mugshots, court records, news photographs) in February 2026. The audit confirmed 100% concordance with the original coding. Additionally, the four-tier bias motive framework was designed to minimize subjective judgment: “Confirmed” requires formal legal action, “No Evidence” requires a documented alternative motive, and “Unknown” is the default for ambiguous cases.

Reproducibility: All case data is derived from publicly accessible sources. Any researcher with access to court records, local news archives, and law enforcement statements can independently verify every data point in this study. The methodology is designed for full reproducibility.

Inclusion/Exclusion Criteria

Included: All individuals listed by name in HRC’s annual fatal violence reports for the years 2015–2024. Excluded: Individuals listed by HRC whose deaths were subsequently determined by medical examiners to be non-homicide (e.g., drug overdose, medical emergency). Two such cases were identified and excluded from all bias motive analysis, yielding N = 302 for bias coding and N = 304 for all other analyses.

Key Departures from Conventional Framing

Several findings in this dataset diverge from the dominant advocacy and media narrative:

  • Most cases are not confirmed hate crimes. Only 3.6% involved confirmed anti-transgender bias. The majority of solved cases involved intimate partner violence, disputes, robbery, sex work encounters, or other circumstances common in general homicide.
  • Suspect demographics mirror victim demographics. The conventional framing implies or states that transgender people are primarily killed by cisgender people motivated by hatred. The data shows that the suspect pool’s racial composition is explained almost entirely by intraracial violence dynamics, not by any race-specific anti-transgender animus.
  • Solve rates are not anomalously low. The 62% average clearance rate is comparable to or slightly above national averages, contradicting claims that law enforcement systematically deprioritizes these cases.
  • Intimate partner violence is the leading identified motive, consistent with national patterns for female homicide generally, rather than bias-motivated stranger violence.

Reframing the Problem

The dominant public narrative about fatal violence against transgender people frames it primarily as a hate crime phenomenon — an “epidemic of violence” driven by anti-transgender animus. This framing has shaped policy responses, funding priorities, and media coverage for more than a decade. The data in this study suggests the reality is more complex and that the hate-crime-centric framing, while politically effective, may be misdirecting resources away from the interventions most likely to save lives.

This matters because misdiagnosing a problem guarantees the failure of every intervention built on that diagnosis. If the leading driver of fatal violence against transgender people is intimate partner violence — disproportionately occurring within Black communities, mirroring the same intraracial patterns seen in all U.S. homicide data — then framing the problem as white supremacist hate violence is not merely inaccurate. It is actively harmful. It directs funding, policy attention, and public awareness toward a cause (societal transphobia, legislative rhetoric) that accounts for a small fraction of documented cases, while ignoring the cause (interpersonal violence, structural poverty, intimate partner abuse) that accounts for the majority.

The analogy is straightforward: if pedestrians are dying at a crosswalk because the signal is broken, but authorities insist the problem is the color of the pavement, no number of pavement-painting campaigns will reduce the death toll. The signal remains broken. People continue to die. And anyone who points to the actual signal malfunction is accused of missing the point.

To be direct: this dataset shows that the predominant pattern of fatal violence against transgender people in the United States involves Black male suspects killing Black transgender women in the context of intimate relationships, sexual encounters, and interpersonal disputes. This is not a comfortable finding. It is not a politically convenient finding. But it is what the case-by-case evidence shows, consistently, across a full decade of data. Refusing to state this plainly — as HRC has done in every annual report, as every presidential proclamation has done, as every congressional resolution has done — does not protect Black transgender women. It abandons them to a cycle of violence that the people and institutions claiming to advocate for them will not even accurately describe.

Honest diagnosis is a precondition of effective intervention. If the goal is to reduce the number of transgender people killed each year, the conversation must begin with the actual data — not with a narrative framework constructed for political utility.

Intimate Partner Violence Should Be the Policy Priority

Intimate partner, domestic, and family violence is the #1 identifiable motive in this dataset. This is consistent with national patterns for female homicide broadly. Policy responses focused exclusively on hate crime legislation and anti-bias training may miss the most common pathway to fatal violence: abusive relationships. Transgender-specific domestic violence services, emergency housing, and relationship safety resources are likely to have a greater marginal impact on reducing fatalities than additional hate crime statutes.

The “Police Violence” Narrative Is Empirically False

A specific sub-narrative within the broader framing deserves its own examination, because it has been advanced by a former President of the United States, the current Mayor of New York City, and dozens of LGBTQ organizations: the claim that transgender people — particularly transgender women of color — are disproportionately killed by law enforcement.

Across this entire 10-year dataset of 304 victims, the number of transgender people killed by law enforcement is 6 — representing 2.0% of all cases. Every single one was ruled a justified use of force. The remaining 98% were killed by civilians: intimate partners, acquaintances, clients, strangers, and family members. There is no “epidemic of police violence” against transgender people in this data. There is not even a statistically detectable pattern.

The most prominent police case on HRC’s list is Tony McDade, and it is worth examining in detail because of what it reveals about the distance between narrative and evidence.

On June 3, 2020, at an Obama Foundation town hall titled “Reimagining Policing in the Wake of Continued Police Violence,” President Barack Obama named Tony McDade alongside George Floyd, Breonna Taylor, and Ahmaud Arbery. Five days later, then-Assembly candidate Zohran Mamdani — now the 112th Mayor of New York City — tweeted that “trans women are disproportionately impacted by police violence.” Over 100 LGBTQ organizations signed an open letter listing McDade as a victim of anti-transgender violence. A GoFundMe raised over $190,000 for his family.

The facts of the case: Tony McDade fatally stabbed 21-year-old Malik Jackson, then pointed a firearm at a responding officer. McDade had posted a Facebook video that morning announcing his intention to kill and predicting suicide by cop: “I’m gonna pull it out and you know these officers nowadays they see a gun they just gonna shoot.” A Leon County grand jury cleared the officer. Police body camera footage confirmed McDade aimed a firearm at the officer before being shot. The family of Malik Jackson told reporters they had to fight to even have their son’s death acknowledged, as activists insisted McDade’s prior actions were irrelevant.

The second most documented police case is Kiwi Herring (2017, St. Louis, MO): Herring stabbed a neighbor (collapsed lung), then slashed a responding police officer with a knife. Officers discharged their weapons at the scene. The officer’s knife wound was documented and treated at a local hospital. The remaining 4 police-involved cases in this dataset follow the same pattern: each involved an armed or violent encounter that preceded the officer’s use of force, and none resulted in criminal charges against the officer.

Summary: Six police-involved killings in ten years. Every one ruled justified. In the cases with detailed records, the decedent had attacked someone with a deadly weapon — a knife, a gun, or both — before police responded with lethal force. Not a single unjustified police killing of a transgender person appears in a decade of data. The claim that transgender people are “disproportionately impacted by police violence” is not a matter of interpretation. It is empirically false. Diverting attention and resources toward a “police violence” framework does not protect transgender lives. It protects a political narrative.

Solve Rates Are Not the Problem

At 62% average clearance, these cases are solved at rates comparable to or above the national average. This contradicts the frequently cited claim that law enforcement ignores or deprioritizes transgender homicides. While individual instances of police indifference certainly exist and are documented in the year reports, the aggregate data does not support a systemic pattern of neglect.

Data Integrity Matters for Advocacy

Advocacy organizations serve a vital role in drawing attention to violence against marginalized communities. However, when advocacy framing departs from empirical evidence — by presuming all deaths are hate-motivated, by eliding suspect demographics, or by claiming systemic law enforcement failure — it undermines credibility and misdirects resources. The most effective advocacy is grounded in accurate data. This study demonstrates that rigorous, case-level analysis can coexist with genuine concern for the safety of transgender people.

Structural Vulnerability, Not Just Bias

The concentration of both victims and suspects among Black Americans, the prevalence of intimate partner violence, and the significant representation of sex work encounters and disputes suggest that structural factors — poverty, housing instability, survival sex work, limited access to safety resources — are at least as important as interpersonal bias in explaining these deaths. Effective prevention requires addressing these structural conditions, not only prosecuting bias after the fact.

Objection 2: “Black People Are Disproportionately Exonerated”

The National Registry of Exonerations has documented 1,167 murder exonerations since 1989. Over 33 years. That comes out to 35 per year nationwide — 19 Black, 11 White, 4 Hispanic. Nineteen Black murder exonerations per year, in a country where the FBI identifies 6,454 Black homicide offenders per year. That is 19 out of 6,454 — a 0.30% exoneration rate. The White rate is 11 out of 4,745, or 0.24%. Nearly identical.

Methodological note: The FBI’s Uniform Crime Reports classify Hispanic offenders under “White” in their race tables. The 4,745 “White” homicide offenders cited above includes an unknown number of Hispanic individuals. This study codes White and Hispanic suspects as separate categories, providing a more granular demographic breakdown than federal crime data allows.

19 exonerations per year. Out of 6,454. That is a 99.7% accuracy rate. The White accuracy rate is 99.76%. The reason 55% of murder exonerations are Black is that 55.9% of known murder offenders are Black. More cases in, more exonerations out. The NRE’s own identified drivers of wrongful murder convictions — police misconduct and cross-racial misidentification — do not apply here. These are overwhelmingly intraracial cases between people who knew each other. There is no cross-racial eyewitness to misidentify anyone.

Objection 3: “What About the Unsolved Cases?”

Of 304 total victims, 186 have an identified suspect with known race. The remaining 118 cases are unsolved — no suspect was ever identified. An objection could hold that these 118 unknowns might have a different racial distribution that would change the finding. This is a fair question, so what follows is a steel-man robustness check: assume the absolute worst case for this study’s central finding and see whether it survives. It does.

Worst case: Assume every single unsolved case was committed by a White perpetrator. This is an absurd assumption, but grant it for the sake of argument. The result: Black suspects would be 122 out of 304, or 40.1% — still 2.9× their 13.6% share of the U.S. population. White suspects would rise from 34 to 152 (50%) — which would still be below their 59.3% share of the U.S. population (0.84×). Even in this impossible scenario, Black Americans would remain overrepresented by a factor of three and White Americans would remain underrepresented.

Expected case: The 118 unsolved victims are approximately 70% Black, matching the overall victim pool. Per FBI Expanded Homicide Data Table 6, roughly 90% of Black homicide victims are killed by Black offenders. Applying national intraracial rates to the unsolved cases would add approximately 74 Black suspects, bringing the total to ~196 out of 304 — 64%, essentially identical to the solved rate of 65%. The unsolved cases, if anything, would reinforce the finding.

Objection 4: “Trans Discovery Violence Should All Be Coded as Hate Crimes”

This study coded 11 cases (3.6%) as confirmed anti-trans bias — cases where hate crime charges were filed or law enforcement explicitly identified an anti-trans motive. An additional 34 cases were coded as “Suspected” bias, a category that includes killings triggered by the perpetrator’s discovery of the victim’s transgender status (“trans panic” or “trans discovery” scenarios). A reasonable objection would argue that every trans discovery killing should be classified as a confirmed hate crime, which could raise the total to approximately 16 or higher. Grant this objection entirely and examine what changes.

The math: Even if all trans discovery cases are reclassified as confirmed bias — and even if every single one of the 34 “Suspected” cases is added, producing a maximum of 45 bias-motivated homicides over ten years — the overall transgender homicide rate remains approximately 1.9 per 100,000, roughly one-third the general U.S. homicide rate of 6.4 per 100,000. At the general population rate, a community of 1.6 million transgender Americans would be expected to experience approximately 102 homicides per year. The actual figure is ~30. The word “epidemic” implies a rate above baseline. The trans homicide rate is below it regardless of how bias is coded. Even under the most expansive possible bias classification, 85% of cases (259 of 304) would still not be bias-motivated, and the dominant circumstance categories — intimate partner violence, sex work encounters, and interpersonal disputes — would remain unchanged.

A note on framing: This study does not minimize the gravity of any homicide, including those triggered by discovery of a victim’s transgender status. These killings are criminal acts and the perpetrators were charged and prosecuted accordingly. However, a methodological question arises: is a killing triggered by the discovery of undisclosed transgender identity during a sexual encounter analytically identical to a premeditated attack motivated by abstract hatred of transgender people? The “trans panic” framing treats these as equivalent. But the interpersonal context matters for accurate motive coding: these cases typically involve a specific deception about sexual identity that the other party experienced as a consent violation — however disproportionate and criminal the violent response. This is not offered as moral justification; it is offered as an analytical distinction between targeted hate violence and reactive interpersonal violence. Collapsing that distinction inflates the hate crime count, obscures the actual dynamics, and misdirects prevention efforts away from the interpersonal contexts where the majority of this violence actually occurs.

Summary: The suspect demographics in this dataset are confirmed by convictions and guilty pleas, consistent across 10 independent years, and mirror national FBI homicide patterns. National exoneration data — 19 per year out of 6,454 Black homicide offenders — shows wrongful conviction rates are nearly identical across races when measured against offending rates (0.30% vs. 0.24%). The expected wrongful convictions in this dataset: less than one. Even assigning all 118 unsolved cases to White perpetrators, Black suspects remain at 40% — three times their population share.

Limitations

This study has several important limitations that should be considered when interpreting the findings:

Single-coder methodology. All case coding was conducted by a single researcher with AI assistance (Claude, Anthropic). While a February 2026 audit confirmed 100% concordance for suspect racial classifications, the absence of traditional multi-coder inter-rater reliability testing is a limitation. The four-tier bias motive framework was designed to minimize subjectivity, but some classifications — particularly the boundary between “Suspected” and “No Evidence” bias — involve interpretive judgment.

Unknown motive cases. Approximately 56% of coded cases were classified “Unknown” for bias motive, driven primarily by unsolved cases with no identified suspect or motive. The true motive distribution may differ from what solved cases suggest. If unsolved cases contain a higher proportion of bias-motivated killings (e.g., stranger attacks that are harder to solve), the 3.6% confirmed bias rate could underestimate the true prevalence.

Geographic gaps. Puerto Rico cases from 2015–2019 are underrepresented due to limited English-language source availability. Spanish-language court records for these years were not systematically reviewed. Puerto Rico cases from 2020–2024, particularly federal hate crime prosecutions, are included.

2017 HTML extraction issue. The 2017 data on documented circumstances could not be fully extracted due to formatting issues in the source HTML. Circumstance coding for 2017 is therefore incomplete, which affects Chart I (Top Circumstances) but does not affect suspect demographics, bias coding, or case status analyses.

Population denominator uncertainty. Disproportionality indices use 2020 Census population shares as denominators. The transgender population is not enumerated by the Census, making rate-per-capita calculations (as opposed to share comparisons) subject to significant uncertainty. Different population estimates (Williams Institute, Census Bureau surveys) yield different rate calculations.

Survivorship bias in source data. HRC’s victim lists rely on media coverage and community reporting. Cases that receive no media coverage or community attention may be systematically excluded. This could bias the dataset toward cases in urban areas, cases involving victims known to LGBTQ communities, or cases that fit the advocacy narrative. The direction and magnitude of this bias is unknown.

Summary of Findings

This study independently verified 304 cases of fatal violence against transgender and gender-non-conforming people in the United States from 2015 to 2024 and produced the first case-level dataset with suspect demographics, bias motive coding, circumstance categorization, and case outcomes across a full decade. The findings contradict the institutional consensus at every major point of analysis:

The suspect pool is not what the narrative claims. Black suspects account for 64.9% of identified perpetrators (χ² = 220.47, p < 0.001; Cramér’s V = 0.63). White suspects account for 18.1% — less than one-third of their population share. In 2020, the year the institutional consensus peaked, zero White suspects were identified. The pattern is consistent across all ten years, confirmed by convictions and guilty pleas, and mirrors national FBI homicide data.

These are not hate crimes. Only 3.6% of coded cases involved a confirmed anti-transgender bias motive. The #1 identified circumstance is intimate partner, domestic, and family violence (21% of known-motive cases), followed by disputes, sex work encounters, and robberies — the same categories that dominate general homicide data.

Law enforcement is not the problem. The 62% average solve rate meets or exceeds the national average. Six police-involved killings were identified in ten years; every one was ruled justified. Not a single unjustified police killing of a transgender person appears in a decade of data.

The violence is intraracial. Approximately 80% of solved cases involve a suspect and victim of the same race. Black-on-Black pairings constitute ~60% of all known pairings. This is consistent with the FBI’s finding that 91% of Black homicide victims are killed by Black offenders. The suspect pool is disproportionately Black because the victim pool is disproportionately Black, and most homicide is intraracial.

Implications for Prevention

If the goal is to reduce the number of transgender people killed each year — not to advance a political narrative, not to build an institutional brand, not to win an election, but to actually prevent human beings from dying — then the data in this study tells you exactly where to intervene:

Intimate partner violence programs. Transgender-specific domestic violence services, emergency housing, safety planning, and relationship support — particularly in Black communities, where the concentration of both victims and perpetrators is highest. This is the #1 identified pathway to fatal violence and the most actionable finding in the dataset.

Accurate data collection and suspect reporting. For over a decade, HRC published the names, photographs, and stories of every victim while never once reporting the demographic identity of the people actually committing these homicides. This omission is not an oversight — it is a choice that has allowed an unsupported narrative to drive policy, legislation, and public understanding. Any serious effort to reduce fatal violence requires honest identification of who is committing it, under what circumstances, and why. Prevention programs cannot be designed around a fictional perpetrator profile. The absence of suspect-level data in every major institutional report on this subject is itself a finding of this study.

Evidence-based interventions, not narrative-based interventions. Not hate crime legislation for a phenomenon that accounts for 3.6% of cases. Not defunding police when 6 police killings in 10 years were all justified. Not “white supremacy” frameworks when zero White suspects were identified in 2020. The interventions that would actually save lives are the same evidence-based approaches that reduce homicide in the general population: violence interruption programs, economic opportunity, housing stability, and domestic violence prevention.

A Final Question

Every person and institution quoted in the Literature Review called for urgent action to protect transgender lives. They demanded legislation, funding, investigations, executive orders, and cultural change. They read victims’ names aloud on debate stages. They signed open letters. They passed resolutions. They declared states of emergency. The question this dataset forces is simple: would they still?

If Sen. Booker knew that the people killing Black transgender women are overwhelmingly Black men — not the white supremacist system he invoked — would he still write that essay in The Advocate? If Rep. Jayapal knew that only 3.6% of these homicides have confirmed bias motivation, would she still introduce a resolution declaring a hate-driven “epidemic”? If the 465 celebrities who signed GLAAD’s open letter knew that the #1 cause of death is intimate partner violence, not the “hostile climate of anti-LGBTQ rhetoric” they condemned, would they still sign? If the AMA knew that solve rates for these cases are at or above the national average, would they still lobby Congress about law enforcement failure? If the scholars building theories of “cis-supremacy” rooted in colonialism knew that the actual data shows intraracial interpersonal violence virtually identical to national homicide patterns, would they still publish those papers? And if all of them knew that transgender people are not killed at rates above the general population but at rates one-third of the general population — that the word “epidemic” they have all used is not merely imprecise but the opposite of what the data shows — would any of them have said anything at all?

Perhaps some would. The structural vulnerabilities are real. The deaths are real. The need for intervention is real. But the question answers itself for most: the institutional energy behind this issue was never primarily about the victims. It was about the narrative. A narrative in which white supremacy, legislative hostility, and societal transphobia are the villains is politically useful across the entire spectrum documented above — from presidential campaigns to academic tenure files to celebrity brand management. A narrative in which Black intimate partners are the primary perpetrators, in patterns indistinguishable from general homicide data, is useful to no one’s political project. And so the narrative won, the data was never examined, and the people who are actually dying — Black transgender women, killed by people they knew, in circumstances that look nothing like a hate crime — were left without the interventions that might have saved them.

The institutional consensus did not fail because the data was unavailable. It failed because no one checked. The data is now public. The excuse is gone.
